The John M. Kuehne Physics Mathematics Astronomy library (AKA PMA library), was housed on the 4th and 5th floors of the PMA building (formerly known as the RLM[^1] building) and as it's namesake implied, carried Physics, Math, and Astronomy books and journals. Despite the run down, brutalist nature of PMA, the library housed within was a point of pride. There are not many other institutions with departmental libraries within their own building, but they are more common among the stereotypical prestigious universities around the world 

<a href="/posts/physics_life/documents/pma_history_rev2023.pdf">
  David Flaxbart and Molly White
</a> have a lovely little article detailing the history of PMA. The physics collection has been around since at least 1900 and has moved several times, each time to bigger and better locations. It was an invaluable resource for students, faculty, and researchers in both the books it housed, study and meeting space, technological resources, and librarians.

## What is this post about?

Over the past few years, there have been a chain of library closures across not just UT, but across the United States as well. This is not disconnected from the broader anti-intellectualism movement in the United States and the axe to academia. There have been several libraries that have closed at UT over the year. One library closure was prevented due to the UT faculty senate. This was not possible for the PMA library as Texas Senate Bill 37 ended the faculty senate. Discussions on the above require more than anecdotes and warrant a separate post. 

The TLDR of events is the following:
> It was suddenly announced that the library would be closing via a vague email.
> 
> Graduate students mobilized to ask why the closure is happening, and what could be done to stop it.
> 
> After feedback from the department head, the graduate students drafted a petition to help spread awareness about the closure. They also contacted the other department heads and dean.
> 
> These efforts were largely unsuccessful, so the graduate students decided to mass check out the books.  This seemed to annoy the high-up's into offering vague concessions.

The reason for this post is to criticize the closure of the PMA library, how the news was delivered, and the departments response to the collective attempts of mobilization of the students and faculty. In our contact with the higher up's, they were usually apathetic, marginally condescending, and sometimes straight up lied to us. At the very least, I hope to document the timeline of events here and general sentiment.

In negotiations with the authorities, although we were persistent, we were cordial and took a calm, rational voice to our grievances.  I will not take this tone in this article. 

The following are purely my opinions, anecdotes, and general feelings about the situation. I will sometimes speculate and sometimes directly refer to the higher ups. As Jacques has said, for the time being, we still have the first amendment. 

## Learning of the closure

On March 24th, 2026 we got an <a href="/posts/physics_life/documents/email.pdf">
  email
</a> about "Upcoming Renovations and Plans for Collection in PMA". It says the following in order:

> They want to create more space for the students to study.
> 
> They want to make workspace for the Center for Gravitational Physics.
> 
> They identified repurposing the 4th and 5th floors of the PMA library would suit the needs above. They claim circulation of materials has been declining so the library will be closed.
> 
> They say the collection will be moved. The popular books will move to the tower and the less used books will move to storage. The staff will be retained.

It is disappointing that such a major decision was buried in the middle of an email instead of the in the subject. It is even more disappointing that we were given such a short notice of the closing. The email makes no mention of when the library will close, but it will turn out to be May 6th 2026. *The librarians themselves learned of the closure through this email.*

There are a few immediate criticisms of this email:

- They claim more study space is needed. There is no evidence for this claim.
- There is plenty of space on the 9th floor for the gravity group. Almost half the offices are empty.
- They claim circulation has been decreasing. They provided no evidence for this claim, even when asked to. We will learn from an anonymous source that this is not true.
- We will learn no faculty nor students were consulted so it is unclear of who they "talked to". A generous interpretation may come from the ERC which I discussed [here](https://yfralam.github.io/posts/physics_life/sod/).
- They say the staff will be retained. Staff have the job of not only organizing, but also managing acquisition of new books. While they claim the current staff will be replaced, it is difficult to believe that those positions will be filled once they leave. In the worst case, the collection will degrade over time. 
- It is not clear what metric will be used to determine "popular". Indeed, one of the most helpful parts of the library is being able to just browse the stacks in relevant sections without even checking books out. This type of usefulness does not show up in the traditional circulation metrics. Me and other researches have at several points, found resources that are useful for research by just browsing. It is difficult to see whether a book will be useful or not before flipping through it, even if you have a table of contents. This is doubly true for books covering obscure topics. I would not have found a book on *Heat kernels and Dirac Operators* without browsing. I wouldn't have even thought such a specific book existed.

Throughout this entire process, we heard several stories of how the university had tried to close the PMA library before. These attempts were thwarted due to opposition by Weinberg himself. Weinberg had objected to the removal of not only the stacks, but even the journals. It is a smear on his legacy that immediately after his death, the library was closed. 

## Attempts to Talk

Immediately after this email went out, the graduate students asked the physics department head to talk after the colloquium the following day. We had a long conversation with him where he defended the decision to close the library. 

He claimed that circulation was declining and the undergraduates needed places to study (as if there weren't enough places already). When we asked him for evidence that the library circulation was going down, he admitted he had not seen the data. We asked him if he could try to get the data and he claimed he would try. I asked if he could keep us posted and send us an email with the data if he could, and just a confirmation that he was unable to obtain or share the data if he could not. We never received any email.

We asked why graduate students were not consulted about a decision that could affect our work directly. It turns out, not only were graduate students not consulted, **the faculty themselves** were not consulted. the physics chair had made the decision himself, or with the approval of the other (math/astronomy) department heads. Whether the other department heads were involved in this decision will turn out to be controversial.

We also asked whether the closure of the library is in any part due to the gravity group needing a new space. Indeed, the physics chair is the head of not just the department, but the gravity group as well. the physics chair claimed the closure of the library was in no way due to the gravity group needing a new home. Just that, he had asked the dean: If the library is going to be closed and renovated anyway, why not give the gravity group offices on a renovated 5th floor? In fact, when the math department head heard of this, we also asked if some money could be reserved for the math department to renovate some classrooms.

So we asked what we could do to have our voices heard. the physics chair suggested gathering graduate student voices and drafting a letter saying how this closure would affect our abilities as a researcher. **We were explicitly told it would not be helpful to criticize the decision, ask for evidence on the claims made, or suggest compromises such as renovating the 5th floor but keeping the stacks on the 4th.**

So we set out to collect voices and started petitioning.

As a last resort, I suggested a mass checkout of books. We had plenty of office space and each person can check out 500 books. While not ideal, it is better than not having something to browse. I think some took this as a plan of protest even though I didn't intend for it to be. It was purely to keep potentially helpful books close by. 



## Petitioning

Soon after we talked to the physics chair, we sent an email out with the petition. Additionally, flyers were printed out and hung all over the PMA. These efforts and subsequent communication were largely due to the tremendous efforts of Deepesh, Nick, and Paro. 

It was immediately clear that UT admin were not happy with us. Despite other flyers being up in the same areas, the flyers with the petition on them kept getting taken down. The math department head sent a stern email claiming he had not approved the descision. In fact, such a claim was "simply false, not marginally so, but completely." This was of great confusion to us based on the physics chair's comments. On subsequent emails, he mentioned that he "considered himself a bookworm" and filled out a library survey on his usage. He found his responses to be worrying and thus, extrapolated.

![PMA petition in trash](/posts/physics_life/img/fly.jpg)

Later on we tried to meet with the math chair. His response was negative. He said he was unsure how investigating the decision making process fit in with our role as a graduate student and that we were mischaracterizing the physics chair's words. He admitted the communication process has not been perfect but "anyone who is willing to look at this matter with a modicum of sense of reality (i.e., acknowledging the lack of spaces for students, the exorbitant costs of creating new ones, and the constantly declining usage of physics copies of books and journals) should conclude that, to very least, this is a very reasonable decision." It would have been nice to have any evidence of these claims. Surely if it is so obvious, the data would be glaringly obvious.

The astronomy chair apparently mentioned that the claim more undergraduates needed space came from the librarians. We talked to the librarians themselves and were unable to confirm this claim. I suppose they could have talked to the library higher ups. More on this later. 

Furthermore, we received an email from the physics chair saying in our initial meeting with him, he never said all 3 PMA chairs were consulted. Just that he was due to plans to renovate the 5th floor for the gravity group. This is in direct contradiction to our conversation with him, of which we had at least a dozen witnesses. 

Now it is strange that he claimed the gravity group moving in had nothing to do with the renovations. For this to happen, the dean and libraries would have had to first decide to close the library without consulting *anyone*, then reach out to the physics chair because they knew his group needed a new home and the library already needed to close. Why else would they not have talked to anyone but the physics chair?

After about 2 weeks of the petition, we got into contact with a former PMA librarian, though still working for UT. They gave us the following information:
- The non-popular books will be kept at Pickle Research Campus.
- The circulation of PMA library was not "low". It has always had a higher circulation rate than other libraries in the system ~15% checked out at any one time which is a lot. There was ~49% circulation rate (~49% of the books had been checked out within the past 5 years). These were the stats when the librarian worked at PMA, but they are the best stats we have.
- The libraries system has never fought back when closures were asked for in order to reduce costs.

Around mid April, the petition was sitting at around 1000 signatures with a combination of undergrads, graduate students, and faculty.

Throughout this process, we tried repeatedly to get in contact with the department heads and dean for face to face conversations. We were relatively unsuccessful. The responses were usually of the form: "We hear your concerns and will take them into account in the future."

Nick drafted an open letter to the department which I thought encapsulated all of the above points and sentiments of graduate students. It is worth a read, even if its only the last few paragraphs.

## Plan B

Around mid April it became abundantly clear that the UT admin were not going to change their mind.  Thus, we began a mass checkout of books from the library. We checked out not only physics books (focusing on QFT, string theory, and condensed matter), but also math books. 

For us, it was much easier to guess what physics books would be useful by just skimming through, but we did not have this option for mathematics. At least for me, I can't tell if a book will be useful or not just by skimming it. Math books can be so hyperspecialized that a useful book may not be useful to my research now, but it may in the future.

For instance, I randomly found this book on bordisms and spectral sequences just by browsing the stacks. I had no idea such a book even existed but it is exactly what I need for my research *right now*. 

![Book for my research](/posts/physics_life/img/bord.jpg)

Anyways, for the purposes of having potentially useful books in close proximity, we checked out more than 2000 books.

![books 1](/posts/physics_life/img/booka.jpg)

![books 2](/posts/physics_life/img/bookb.jpg)


Andreas was nice enough to hand me a spare office in order to temporarily store some of these books. The reaction from a lot of the other faculty were very positive and more than one said that they were proud of what we were doing. 

Taking out this many books is of course a financial risk. Replacing a book costs on order of $100 so we are on the hook for roughly $200,000 worth of books. To keep track of these books, and who we allow to borrow, we established a catalogue of books. For now the catalogue is an excel sheet but it would be nice to eventually have an established database.

![catalog of books](/posts/physics_life/img/cat.png)

When I was at Osaka U. (Now University of Osaka), there was a high energy theory reading room with thousands of books. It is nice to have something similar now, even if it is split among our offices.
## Denouement

At the current moment, it seems like things are calming down. We were unsuccessful in preserving the PMA library. In hindsight, the admin was extremely smart in how they handled the situation. By informing us about the decision so late, there was almost nothing we could do. As graduate students, we always have the nuclear option of striking but the consensus was this is not the hill to die on. 

The books stored on the 9th floor are quite helpful, but really only a band aid fix. Eventually people will graduate and these books will have to return to the shelves of a campus several miles away from the university. 

Admin was *very* annoyed with us checking out books. We have on record that the department chair was *looking* for any ways we were breaking the laws or code of conduct. It is telling that in addition to the poor communication, contradictions, and condescension, they were looking for ways to get us into trouble. The chair mentioned he looked on whether we were borrowing too many books; we were not. He also mentioned looking into whether letting others borrow our books broke any rules; they do not. At a recent meeting, he claimed that we tricked Andreas into giving us office space to store the books by not telling him what we needed it for; this is completely incorrect, *I* was the one who asked Andreas and I specifically mentioned the books.

Furthermore, we received criticism for our petition; they claimed it came off more as a manifesto. The undergrads had put together compromises for what could be done to the library and this is what they were looking for. This was a surprise to us as we were explicitly told to just gather sentiments and not try and offer compromises.

There is one thing that came of plan B: The department is "considering" giving us a reading room, maybe with the budget for less than 1000 books that are most helpful for our research. I do not understand this point. We already have the books we know would be helpful for us. The reason we collected so many books is to account for the books we may need that we *don't yet know are helpful.* I really don't know who this reading room is for. 

As of now, admin seems to be willing to hear feedback on how best to renovate the 4th floor, i.e. what should be added, how best to use the space. This seems like a massive red flag. They got rid of the library without even having fleshed out plans for what to do with the space? The only thing they know for certain is that the gravity group will get offices on the 5th floor. 

It is very difficult for me to believe that the department chair, also the head of the gravity group, did not go to the dean and ask for the library to be closed so that the gravity group could have office space. Now they need to figure out what to do with the 4th floor. The libraries put up no fight as this reduces their cost, and it is also plausible the other department heads are not consulted. 

There are too many inconsistencies. In the best case scenario, people are bending the truth beyond all recognition; in the most believable scenario, they are lying.
## Epilogue

As of now, there are some faculty (Marder) that are championing us to the dean. Maybe we get a reading room, but I doubt how helpful it will be. We still have no idea what is going to happen to the 4th floor or what "study space" even entails. The chemistry library and engineering library also closed for study space in the same way some years back, no work has been done to them and they sit empty as of now. I expect the same to happen to PMA.

The renovations are quoted as taking "a year", but with no concrete plans of what will happen, I very much doubt this. To rub salt into the would, the department chair will be taking Weinberg's office on the 9th floor. 

In any case, PMA is slated to be demolished. If there is one thing that comes out of this, it would be very nice to have a small library or reading room in the new building. 

I will keep this post updated with any details and issue any corrections if need be. For now, I think it is worth quoting Nick's conclusion directly here:

>The library books should not be removed. We are reluctant to make any concessions, and we would be swallowing a spoonful of pure salt if we were to presuppose that we should bargain down just because we might think that this decision is not reversible. It is true that people in the PMA community are not so opposed to the removal of the journal catalogues to the Life Sciences library or Pickle Research Center as they are to the rest of the books. We are opposed, however, to the reasons behind the decision as you have laid them out, and the manner in which it was made, being that it was done without consulting the PMA community at large, and as I write this, there are well over 1000 signatures to a petition expressing exactly this. 
>
>In the same way that the decision to eliminate gender and ethnic studies departments was made against the will of people in those departments while voices were suppressed and silenced, we find that this is connected in ideology and method with your decision to close the PMA library and that this is related in the same way to the closures of other libraries across campus. It is disturbing that the former of these decisions is one which serves the political interests of the far-right conservative Texas government. 
>
>Education should not be subject to decisions geared toward efficiency in the same way that a business would be. The stifling grind of university lies in contrast to the freedom offered by the library, where one can choose of their own accord the subjects that they wish to study, away from the rigid regulations, deadlines, and cutoffs of classrooms. In an age where one is increasingly drawn away from real life and towards screens, they lose interaction with the world around them; experiences on the internet are not ours, they are owned and controlled by our ISP's and tech corporations. We would be relinquishing autonomy and ownership of our experiences and agency in our education for "efficiency". 
>
>Continual decision-making from the standpoint of efficiency can only result in radical purging of essential resources. If future policy makers employ your logic, then one wonders what will happen to the books after they are moved from the building. Circulation will inevitably decrease as they become increasingly inaccessible, and when it drops to zero, they would be perceived as a waste of space no matter where they were put; at that rate we might as well burn them now and save ourselves the trouble of moving them.


What frustrated me the most is that almost every faculty we talked to all agreed we were morally right, the library shouldn't close. Despite this, the decision has been made and this is how university politics works. But then what is the point of morals if no one acts on them?
