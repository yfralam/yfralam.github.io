## On invitations

A few months ago, there was talk of Eric Weinstein coming to UT to give a "talk" on Geometric Unity. A few students were asked whether we would be interested in attending a seminar (which was met with broad resistance) or at least a small meeting with him. Some others and I were vehemently opposed to any _formal_ interaction with Weinstein.

If you don't know who [Eric Weinstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eric_Weinstein) is, it is enough to know that he is a wealthy investor working for Thiel Capital, graduated with a PhD in math back in 1992, and is largely critical of the physics institution and even more so on high energy theory. He claims to have a "theory of everything" called Geometric Unity[^3] which has been met with extreme skepticism within the physics community.

Weinstein has made a career out of bemoaning the lack of interest or outright animosity towards his "theory" and out of heavily criticizing the physics community.  He has appeared on podcasts —most notably Joe Rogan—interviews, and has rubbed elbows with financial and political elites. 

While nothing was scheduled, just two weeks ago he ended up coming and exchanging some words with people in the department. While those in the department have a right to talk to whoever they want to ("It's a free country for the time being," as Jacques said), any framing where Weinstein is given credibility in the physics community is largely detrimental to physics in the US.

## The fundamental issues

This post isn't so much a critique of Weinstein's theory, plenty of that exists already.[^1] The theory starts in 14 dimensions and introduces structural problems[^4] before any contact with the real world is reached. But this post is more so about his brand. 

My primary point is, Weinstein refuses to share his work by proper channels and has no desire to even have a good faith discussion. He has a "draft" that he refuses to post to arxiv (or even anywhere easily accessible), let alone submit for publication. To access it, one has to sign up for his newsletter on his website.  

This is not how we do science. Modern science requires technical scrutiny and explicit calculations, without them, there is nothing to evaluate.

Indeed, before his draft came out, he spent most of his time talking about his theory in interviews and debates where he comes across as meaningfully obtuse at best, and completely incoherent at worst. In any case, interviews and debates are not the venue to conduct scientific analysis.

In his recent, [rare debate with a physicist](https://youtu.be/5m7LnLgvMnM?si=eKdC_N2JIpHzhTcu), Sean Carroll, Weinstein's only criticisms of Sean are personal attacks. He is downright rude the entire time and discusses practically no physics whatsoever in this or any forum he is part of. Sean makes excellent points for how ridiculous Weinstein's scientific conduct is, not to mention, critiques the paper which Weinstein didn't think he had. 

He often acts as if he is being persecuted by the community and that ideas outside the mainstream (string theory being mainstream, for instance) are shut down. This is not true. While not as popular, there can be (and is) good faith discussions between different ideas (say loop quantum gravity, asymptotic safety, causal dynamical triangulation, etc.). 

Each of these programs has its own issues, but they are issues the community can at least talk about. For example, loop quantum gravity must address the violation of unitarity in graviton scattering, but it is an issue we know about because there are papers published. Due to this, we can at least invite loop quantum gravity speakers (Ashtekar is coming to UT tomorrow[^2]) and ask him technical questions.
## Some justifications for inviting him

There are some justifications I have received for possibly inviting him.

> He's a powerful person and tied to one of the richest venture capitalists in the US.
- It is unclear how entertaining Weinstein would be beneficial to high energy theory. It's not like he has a history of funding hep-th people or groups that humor him. In fact, doing so would probably hurt his brand as someone who goes against the academic establishment.
- There is also the point that we are only talking to him because he has money.  

> He has a large platform and is incredibly popular.
- This depends on your definition of "large". His most successful appearance is on Joe Rogan, but Rogan's audience is there for him, not his guests, and will watch regardless of whoever Rogan has on.  Weinstein's podcast itself does not seem to be that popular, the videos are all from 5+ years ago. Granted some of them have ~100k views, but in the grand scheme of things, this is nothing compared to other sciency channels (PBS spacetime has roughly 1M views per video). 
- It is true that he has appeal with some powerful people but for reasons seen further down, this is precisely related to why we shouldn't platform him. 

> The community has treated him unfairly and we should humor him to turn down the temperature.
- Again, the burden of evidence that he has a good theory is on him. To clear it, he must at least make the paper available. His idea that because his theory is not string theory, hence no one is taking it seriously is patently false. 
- Going around saying "Quantum gravity is a mental disorder" is not turning down the temperature.

> Isn't he spreading his ideas if he wants to give talks on his theory?
- Science is not done through 1 hour talks, let alone debates. It advances inch by inch through detailed calculations and technical scrutiny. When physicists give seminars, there is an implicit understanding that the mathematics (or data) exists and can be produced as needed. For en entirely new framework, the burden of proof[^5] is enormous.

## What's the harm?

The issue with platforming Weinstein is that he will use this invitation as prestige and legitimacy. Already in his interview with Sean, he uses the fact that he gets invited to universities in order to get onto these debates and large podcasts in the first place. On these interviews **which he got by leveraging his invites to universities**, he then peddles that fundamental physics is bankrupt.

Weinstein has not demonstrated interest in anything besides his theory so it is difficult to see how platforming him has any benefit to UT, high energy theory, or physics in general. With his falsely cultivated academic legitimacy, he can pressure politicians and interviewers to care about what he has to say. Historically, this has been negative sentiments about the community.

As a final argument for why Weinstein should not be platformed, taken seriously, and that he refuses to engage in meaningful scientific discussion, I will quote from Weinstein himself: 

>**"The Author is not a physicist and is no longer an active academician, but is an Entertainer and host of The Portal podcast. This work of entertainment is a draft of work in progress which is the property of the author and thus may not be built upon, renamed, or profited from without express permission of the author."**
>	- Geometric Unity: Author's Working Draft, v 1.0 
>	- *Dated April 1st, 2021*
